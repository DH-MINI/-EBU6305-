Director: Wang Shuchen, Yan Dingxian, Ada

    Ne Zha Fights the Sea' is China's first widescreen animated feature film, based on classical literature, which tells the story of the feud between Li Nezha, the son of Li Jing of the Chentang Pass, and the Dragon Palace of the East China Sea.

    The film is a magnificent, well-produced film that features richly drawn characters, a rhythmic narrative, and a fascinating use of colour, with the words 'strange, superb, magnificent, and beautiful' running through every aspect of its creation.

  • 1979: Culture Ministry Excellent Film Award (China)
  • 1983: Special Award at the Manila International Film Festival (Philippines)
  • 1988:
    • Jury Award at the Bourges International Festival of Young Animated Films (France)
    • Wide-Screen Feature Animation Award at the Bourges International Festival of Young Animated Films (France)
  • 1980: Best Animated Film Award at the 3rd Hundred Flowers Awards (China)

Ne Zha's Uprising in the Dragon Palace: A Tale of Rebellion and Redemption
The Incident
  • Cause: Ne Zha accidentally kills the Dragon King's son, Ao Bing.
  • Confrontation: Ne Zha bravely confronts the Dragon King and his army.
  • Resolution: Ne Zha defeats the Dragon King's forces but sacrifices himself to save his people.
Significance
  • Character Development: Ne Zha's actions showcase his impulsiveness, rebellious spirit, and loyalty.
  • Themes: The story explores themes of rebellion, justice, consequences, responsibility, and redemption.
  • Cultural Impact: The story has become a popular legend, inspiring adaptations in literature, opera, and film.

The Enduring Educational Significance and Influence of the Ne Zha Story
Educational Significance
  • Moral Lessons: Teaches valuable lessons about responsibility, consequences, and facing mistakes.
  • Cultural Values: Reflects traditional Chinese values like filial piety, loyalty, and pursuit of justice.
  • Self-Discovery and Identity: Resonates with young people navigating adolescence and identity formation.
Cultural Influence
  • Literature and Art: Inspired countless adaptations in Chinese literature, opera, and film.
  • Popular Culture: Ne Zha's image and characteristics have become widely recognized.
  • Social Commentary: Used as a vehicle for social commentary, addressing prejudice, discrimination, and acceptance.

Ne Zha's Self-Immolation in Chentangguan: A Moment of Sacrifice and Redemption
The Decision
  • Realization of Consequences: Ne Zha understands the potential devastation his conflict has caused.
  • Acceptance of Responsibility: Ne Zha acknowledges his actions have consequences and chooses to bear the burden.
  • Ultimate Sacrifice: Ne Zha severs his own flesh and bones, returning them to his parents as a final act of filial piety.
Significance
  • Character Development: Ne Zha's self-immolation showcases his growth, maturity, and understanding of consequences.
  • Themes: The story explores themes of sacrifice, redemption, and the power of love.
  • Cultural Impact: Ne Zha's self-immolation has become a poignant and memorable moment in Chinese mythology.
